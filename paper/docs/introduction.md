# Introduction

## Context and Problem

Process mining derives knowledge about operational processes from *event logs*: collections of timestamped, labeled events that are grouped into cases. The discipline rests on a strong implicit assumption, namely that the process under study leaves digital traces in an information system (a workflow engine, an ERP, an MES, a hospital information system) from which an event log can be extracted. Four decades of enterprise digitization have made this assumption reasonable for administrative and transactional processes, and the success of process mining in domains such as procure-to-pay, order-to-cash, and healthcare pathways is directly tied to it.

The assumption breaks down, however, precisely where a large share of the world's value creation still happens: on the *manual shop floor*. In labor-intensive manufacturing (stamping, casting, garment production, manual assembly, packaging) the operationally decisive behavior consists of physical actions performed by human workers: picking a part from a bin, loading a blank into a die, actuating a press, inspecting a seam, carrying a full container to a consolidation point, waiting for a coworker. These actions are usually not recorded by any information system. At best, an MES logs order-level or machine-level checkpoints, leaving the actual work (its sequence, its duration, its interruptions, its material-handling overhead) invisible. Kratsch et al. aptly describe such manual activities as the *blind spots* of process mining [KRATSCH2022]. As long as event data stops at the system boundary, process mining cannot answer questions such as: *How much of a worker's time is value-adding versus transport and replenishment? Where do micro-stoppages and rework occur? Do workers performing the same task follow the same procedure? Which workstation layouts induce unnecessary motion?*

A complementary data source has recently become available at scale: *egocentric video*. Head-mounted cameras record the shop floor from the worker's own perspective, capturing hand-object interactions, machine operation, material flows, and movements through the facility with a level of detail that neither fixed cameras (occlusion, distance, limited field coverage) nor enterprise systems can match. The Egocentric-10K dataset [^1] exemplifies this development: it contains thousands of hours of first-person factory footage recorded by more than two thousand workers in real industrial facilities. Such corpora are collected primarily to train robotics and vision models, but they constitute (in principle) an extraordinarily rich observational substrate for process analysis: every video is a faithful, continuous, fine-grained record of a process execution.

[^1]: https://huggingface.co/datasets/builddotai/Egocentric-10K

The problem addressed in this paper is the transformation gap between these two worlds: **raw egocentric video is not event data**. A video is an unstructured stream of pixels; an event log requires discretized events with activity labels drawn from a coherent vocabulary, timestamps, and case identifiers. Bridging this gap manually is prohibitively expensive (annotating a single hour of video with fine-grained activity segments takes a trained annotator many hours) and it does not scale to the thousands of hours that make such corpora interesting in the first place.

## The Gap

Three research streams approach this problem from different sides, and none of them closes it.

First, *video-based process mining* has demonstrated that traces can be extracted from video for discovery and conformance checking [KNOCH2020, KRATSCH2022, LEPSIEN2023, CHEN2023]. However, this line of work relies predominantly on fixed, exocentric cameras observing a single workstation, on computer-vision models trained per setting (object detectors, pose estimators, activity classifiers tailored to one assembly task), and on small-scale lab or single-station studies. The per-setting training effort effectively replaces one manual bottleneck (annotation) with another (model engineering), and the fixed viewpoint restricts analysis to one station rather than following the worker through the process.

Second, *sensor-based process mining* [MANNHARDT2018, KOSCHMIDER2020, JANSSEN2021, BRZYCHCZY2025] has developed abstraction techniques for lifting low-level signals to process-level events, but wearable and ambient sensors capture far less semantic content than video: a wrist accelerometer cannot distinguish *loading a blank into a die* from *placing a finished part into a bin*.

Third, *LLM-assisted event log construction* has shown that large language models can extract event logs from unstructured or semi-structured text such as documents, conversations, and database schemas [DANI2025, GEEGANAGE2024, KECHT2021]. This stream has not yet been connected to video as the originating modality, and it leaves open the questions that dominate in the video setting: how to segment a continuous activity stream into events, how to assign timestamps that were never recorded by any system, and how to control the vocabulary so that logs from different workers and recordings remain comparable and minable.

What is missing is (i) a method that turns *egocentric* factory video into standard process mining event logs **without any per-setting model training and without manual annotation**, (ii) a publicly available derived dataset of realistic scale, ready for process mining, on which the community can study such logs, and (iii) an honest, multi-level assessment methodology that quantifies what such automatically generated logs are (and are not) good for.

## Proposed Solution

We propose and instantiate a fully automated, five-stage pipeline that transforms egocentric factory videos into process mining event logs, using modern foundation models as its only extraction machinery:

1. **Video transcription.** A multimodal foundation model converts each video into a detailed *chronological, timestamped textual narration* of observable behavior. The prompt deliberately requests plain descriptive text (no labels, no schema) and enforces change-point-based segmentation: a new timestamped entry is opened only when the action, object, location, or social context changes, while stable repetitive stretches are compressed into single intervals.
2. **Worker-level summarization.** All transcriptions of one worker are synthesized into a process-focused report describing the processes the worker performs, their operational flow, and visible optimization opportunities.
3. **Factory-level vocabulary construction.** The worker reports of each factory are aggregated into a factory report that includes two *controlled vocabularies*: a short list of **process labels** (e.g., *metal stamping*, *garment bagging*) and a rich catalogue of fine-grained **activity labels** in verb-object form (e.g., *load raw metal blank into die*, *actuate machine press*). These vocabularies act as a domain-specific abstraction layer, fixed per factory.
4. **Constrained event extraction.** Each raw transcription is converted into a sequence of events (with estimated start and end timestamps, an activity label, and a process label) where both labels are *strictly constrained* to the factory vocabularies. This closed-world constraint is the central design decision of the pipeline: it suppresses label hallucination, harmonizes granularity, and makes events comparable across videos, workers, and analysis runs.
5. **Log consolidation.** Per-video event lists are merged into worker-level and factory-level CSV event logs with explicit case identifiers, relative and absolute timestamps, and durations, directly loadable into standard process mining tooling.

Applying the pipeline to six factories of Egocentric-10K yields a derived corpus of **322 annotated videos from 59 workers, 13,304 timestamped transcript entries, and 10,698 events** over controlled vocabularies of 55-131 activity labels and 12-24 process labels per factory, covering more than 100 hours of observed work. Both the pipeline (scripts and prompts) and the derived artifacts are released publicly.

We deliberately position the resulting logs as *inferred observational data*, not as ground truth. Every event is the product of two model-mediated interpretation steps (video→text, text→events), and its timestamps, boundaries, and labels carry uncertainty. A substantial part of this paper is therefore devoted to an assessment framework that makes this uncertainty measurable, spanning structural validity, vocabulary conformance, temporal fidelity, semantic fidelity, and downstream process mining utility.

## Research Questions

The paper addresses the following research questions:

- **RQ1 (Feasibility and validity).** Can a foundation-model pipeline, without task-specific training or human annotation, transform egocentric factory video into event logs that satisfy the structural requirements of process mining (well-formed timestamps, ordered events, and activity/process labels that conform to a controlled vocabulary) at a scale of hundreds of videos?
- **RQ2 (Fidelity).** How faithfully do the generated events reflect the observed work? This decomposes into *temporal fidelity* (which share of the observed footage is accounted for by events, and how well do event boundaries align with observed behavioral change points) and *semantic fidelity*, whether the assigned activity and process labels correctly describe what happens in the corresponding time interval, and whether operationally relevant behavior is missed.
- **RQ3 (Process mining utility).** To what extent do the resulting logs support standard process mining analyses (control-flow discovery, variant analysis, performance analysis) and at which abstraction level (fine-grained activities versus process phases) do discovery algorithms produce useful models for this class of highly repetitive, flexibly ordered manual work?

## Contributions

The contributions of this paper are:

1. A **method**: a five-stage, prompt-engineered, foundation-model pipeline for deriving controlled-vocabulary event logs from egocentric process-execution videos, requiring no per-setting training and no manual annotation (Section *Approach*).
2. An **artifact**: a publicly released derived dataset (transcriptions, worker and factory process reports, factory-specific activity/process vocabularies, and per-video, per-worker, and per-factory event logs) for six real factories from Egocentric-10K.
3. An **assessment framework and empirical evaluation**: a five-level evaluation methodology for video-derived event logs (structural validity, vocabulary conformance, temporal fidelity, semantic fidelity, and process mining utility) together with its complete application to the corpus, including a manual audit of event correctness and controlled discovery experiments at two abstraction levels (Section *Assessment*).
4. A **characterization of failure modes** (most notably the collapse of temporal coverage on very long recordings and the precision limits of discovery on fine-grained manual-work logs) that defines a concrete research agenda for video-derived event data.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. The *Related Work* section positions the contribution with respect to event log construction, video/sensor-based process mining, and LLM-assisted process mining. The *Approach* section details the pipeline and its design rationale. The *Assessment* section defines the evaluation framework and reports the empirical results. The *Discussion* section examines limitations, threats to validity, and ethical considerations of worker-centered video analysis, and the *Conclusion* outlines future work, including object-centric extensions and video-grounded validation.
