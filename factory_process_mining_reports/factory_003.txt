Factory Process Overview

The factory executes a diverse mix of highly manual surface preparation, intricate sub-assembly, and semi-automated molding operations across multiple material types, including plastics, metals, wax casting patterns, ceramics, and wooden mechanisms. The prevailing manufacturing strategy relies heavily on manual defect patching, flash removal, and the hand-application of various pastes, adhesives, and coatings. Workflows are characterized by frequent transitions between single-piece and batched flows, high tool-switching frequency, and significant localized material handling. While core shaping processes utilize injection molding and machine pressing, the bulk of the factory's labor hours are dedicated to detailed pre-processing (mold cleaning, insert staging) and post-processing (deburring, surface sealing, component joining) before finished parts are transported to centralized storage racks.

Optimization-Relevant Process Observations

* Material Handling and Transport Waste: Workers across multiple stations frequently halt value-adding operations to carry heavy metal trays, push wheelbarrows, or drag bins across the factory floor to storage aisles. Implementing localized gravity flow racks, roller conveyors, or assigning dedicated material handlers would significantly reduce non-value-added motion and transport times.
* Ergonomics and Material Presentation: Raw materials and unworked parts are consistently dumped into disorganized desktop piles or kept in deep cardboard boxes on the floor. This forces repetitive bending, reaching, and visual searching. Elevating material storage to workbench height and installing gravity-fed hoppers or point-of-use bins would streamline the grasping phase and reduce ergonomic strain.
* Tool Clutter and Changeover Friction: High-frequency manual finishing tasks require workers to constantly switch between scrapers, brushes, files, cloths, and air guns. The lack of standardized tool holsters or shadow boards leads to cluttered workbenches, increasing the micro-delays associated with locating, picking up, and setting down implements.
* Air-Blowing Cross-Contamination: The ubiquitous use of compressed air guns to clear metal shavings, wood dust, and wax debris directly adjacent to open containers of wet adhesives, paints, and putties introduces a severe risk of particulate cross-contamination. Transitioning to localized vacuum extraction or downdraft tables would capture waste at the source without dispersing it into neighboring workspaces.
* Batching Variability and Machine Idle Time: Workers operating injection and pressing machines often leave the equipment idle while they manually scrape or trim large batches of extracted parts. Conversely, during closed-mold cycles, workers sometimes wait idly. Synchronizing manual deflashing, core prepping, or cleaning tasks to occur strictly within the automated machine cycle time would optimize overall throughput.
* Manual Dispensing Inefficiencies: The manual application of putties and pastes using bare fingers, loose brushes, or plastic squeeze bags results in inconsistent application volumes and frequently necessitates secondary hand-cleaning pauses. Introducing foot-pedal-operated pneumatic dispensers or standardized dosing syringes would improve application precision and reduce process mess.
