Factory Process Overview
The facility operates as a highly manual garment manufacturing and packaging plant. The production flow encompasses initial bulk fabric preparation (spreading, layering, and cutting), multi-stage industrial sewing (overlock seaming, binding, hemming, and complex curvilinear joining), garment customization via heat press labeling, and end-of-line manual finishing operations. Finishing includes thread trimming, spot cleaning, steam ironing with folding guides, individual polybagging, bulk box assembly, and pick-and-pack order sorting. Across the factory, the workflow is characterized by disconnected workstations, heavy reliance on manual material transport between stations, unstandardized point-of-use material presentation, and operator-led machine maintenance and quality inspection.

Optimization-Relevant Process Observations
- Machine Idle Time and Tool Switching: Operators frequently interrupt high-speed machine sewing to manually cut continuous fabric chains, snip connecting threads, and perform intermediate fabric trimming. The lack of automatic under-bed thread trimmers or machine-mounted guillotines severely limits equipment utilization.
- Material Handling Burden: Point-of-use staging is highly disorganized across the facility. Operators constantly bend down to floor mats, untangle raw materials from unstructured piles, or pull unorganized fabric from distant table edges, creating continuous micro-delays before any value-adding work begins.
- Decoupled Workstations and Transport Waste: The factory relies extensively on operators physically walking heavy fabric bundles, finished shirt stacks, and full bins across the floor to the next station. The absence of localized material handlers (water spiders), conveyor systems, or rolling carts forces skilled machine operators into transport roles.
- Batching Behavior and Workspace Clutter: Operations involving continuous chaining quickly cause massive material accumulation. Because output catchment (bins or chutes) is absent, heavy chains and WIP overflow onto workstations and directly onto operators' laps, requiring them to halt production to push inventory out of the way.
- Redundant Handling and Inspection: Quality routines involve significant double-handling, notably the 100% manual unfolding and refolding of garments solely to verify heat press transfers, and the secondary transferring of already-packaged garments from one floor box to another during sorting. 
- Ergonomic Strain: Pick-and-pack order distribution and scrap material collection are performed directly on the floor, necessitating repetitive bending and deep reaching.

Evidence Limits
- The egocentric and occasionally erratic camera angles obscure the broader factory layout, making it impossible to map precise distances between workstations, evaluate total material travel time, or fully assess global layout inefficiencies.
- The overarching production control logic (e.g., push vs. pull systems, batch scheduling) is entirely unseen. It is uncertain whether the frequent task-switching and movement of workers across different stations are driven by cross-training mandates, ad-hoc supervisor instructions, or floating bottleneck mitigation.
- Specific quality thresholds and defect criteria are unstated, leaving uncertainty regarding exactly when a garment requires manual thread trimming versus when it is acceptable to pass downstream.
- Due to the lack of audio context and clear screen visibility, the exact nature of worker coordination, numeric calculations on smartphones, and handwritten production logbook data cannot be definitively evaluated.
