Factory Process Overview

The factory executes a highly manual, multi-stage garment and textile manufacturing workflow. Operations are broadly categorized into three main phases: raw fabric preparation and sub-assembly, garment finishing, and final packaging and shipping. Fabric preparation involves untangling, flattening, pattern matching, pinning, and batch-marking pre-cut fabric panels. The finishing phase requires workers to iron, fold, and tag individual garments, often utilizing varying manual techniques, temporary templates, or cardboard inserts. The packaging and shipping phase encompasses a complex mix of individual garment bagging, cardboard sleeving, master bagging, and bulk box assembly. Throughout the facility, there is a heavy reliance on worker-led material transport, localized floor staging, and dynamic adjustments to batching, with operators frequently pausing primary value-added tasks to manage their own raw material replenishment, tool maintenance, and finished goods conveyance.

Optimization-Relevant Process Observations

* Material Handling and Operator Travel: There is a severe lack of decoupled material handling. Operators consistently stop primary value-adding operations (folding, tagging, bagging) to walk across the factory floor to retrieve raw materials, fetch unbanded cardboard, gather bulk pens, or transport finished sacks and boxes. Introducing dedicated water spiders or point-of-use gravity flow racks would drastically increase machine and operator utilization.
* Makeshift Tool Usage and Deficiencies: Workers deploy inefficient workarounds due to missing or inadequate tooling. Examples include using a pen tip to cut shipping tape, using a pen to poke holes in paper wraps to secure them, holding loose tagging pins in the palm rather than using a dispenser, and sharing standard desktop tape dispensers across workstations. Standardizing ergonomic point-of-use tools (tape guns, staplers, dedicated scissors) would eliminate these micro-delays.
* Ergonomics and Floor-Level Staging: Vertical motion waste is highly prevalent. Workers repeatedly assemble boxes on the floor, stage fabric on floor boards, drop finished bags into floor-level bins, and bend down to retrieve spilled inventory. Elevating staging areas, using spring-loaded pallet positioners, or implementing tilted presentation carts would eliminate repetitive bending and accelerate cycle times.
* Standardization and Method Variations: High process variation exists both within and between workstations. Operators frequently toggle between single-piece flow and batch-accumulation flow (e.g., edge-marking fabric). Similarly, garment finishing lacks standard operating procedures, with workers randomly alternating between template folding, insert folding, freehand folding, and redundant ironing sequences. Standardizing these methods to the single most efficient technique would stabilize output and reduce rework.
* Two-Person Dependencies and Handoffs: Box assembly acts as a bottleneck because it frequently requires two operators—one to physically hold the box flaps shut and another to apply tape. Providing simple weighted jigs or mechanical case-erectors would decouple these resources, allowing single-piece box assembly and freeing up the second worker for active packing tasks.

Evidence Limits

* The egocentric and tightly framed camera angles frequently obscure the broader factory layout, making it impossible to map the complete end-to-end routing of intermediate sub-assemblies, bulk carts, or finished boxes.
* The explicit purpose of various manual markings (chalk lines, pen ticks on labels, hole pokes in paper) and the contents of the physical administrative paperwork (notebooks, delivery slips, signed documents) are illegible or out of frame.
* Process variations—such as switching between plastic sleeving and cardboard folders, tying hangers versus clipping them, or using permanent inserts versus removable templates—cannot be definitively attributed to specific client SKUs, varying product standards, or mere operator preference based on the visual evidence alone.
* The exact causes for erratic pacing, brief walks away from the station, and short undocumented interactions with coworkers cannot be confidently assessed without audio context or a wider view of the surrounding operations.
