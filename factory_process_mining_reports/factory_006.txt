Factory Process Overview
The factory encompasses diverse manufacturing and material processing operations, primarily focusing on metal sand-casting, compression molding of powdered materials, chemical dough extrusion, wooden component assembly, and metal part finishing. Across the facility, production is highly manual and relies heavily on batch processing, manual part transport, and floor-based staging. Core operations include the preparation and pressing of sand molds (both manual and machine-assisted), the hand-placement of intricate pattern inserts, chemical mixing and kneading, punch pressing, and the manual separation of metal casting scrap. Operational flow is frequently punctuated by manual material handling tasks, such as shoveling raw materials, transporting heavy components via wheelbarrows or hoists, and organizing finished goods into extensive floor arrangements.

Optimization-Relevant Process Observations
- Floor-based operations (part sorting, wooden part assembly, hammering metal scrap, casting separation) force workers into constant bending, sitting, and standing cycles, introducing poor ergonomics and extending batch transfer times.
- Excessive manual material handling acts as a facility-wide bottleneck; heavy reliance on loading wheelbarrows, bulk-dumping onto floors, and manual transport of block stacks introduces significant non-value-added motion and risk of product damage.
- Loose materials handled via hand-scooping and manual shoveling (sand, dough, powders) result in high spillage rates, forcing workers to interrupt primary machine cycles to sweep, reclaim, or restock materials.
- Significant cycle time is consumed by meticulously arranging components (such as aligning metal casings into grids or dragging them into perfectly straight floor rows), which is often followed by bulk dumping, indicating potential rework or highly inefficient staging protocols.
- Machine idle time is visible across multiple pressing operations where workers wait for automated compression or filling cycles to complete, suggesting a need for dual-mold parallel processing or parallel workstation loading.
- High physical friction exists at handoff and tooling points, such as workers constantly donning and doffing gloves to handle tiny mold inserts, or utilizing hand hammers rather than pneumatic tools to break metal casting scrap.
- Track indexing for molds often requires workers to leave their stations to manually pull heavy chains, hooks, or drag molds via metal bars, halting value-adding assembly processes.

Evidence Limits
- Camera angles frequently obscure internal machine mechanisms, exact control panel readouts, specific part details, and the full extent of overhead crane operations.
- The chemical compositions, temperatures, curing times, and safety requirements of the liquids, sprays, chemical doughs, and casting sands cannot be visually verified.
- The downstream routing of finished and semi-finished materials—such as where the perfectly aligned floor rows of casings are moved next, or the destination of finished sand molds—is off-camera.
- Certain counter-intuitive activities (e.g., painstakingly building a grid of parts on a table only to sweep them back onto the floor, or double-pressing a mold without apparent material changes) lack visible context.
- It is unclear if the documented tasks represent a single continuous shift, a specific batch run, or operations captured across different production days.
- The specific quality inspection criteria for the sorting of small metal brackets and plates cannot be determined.
