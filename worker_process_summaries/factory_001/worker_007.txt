Process Summary

The worker performs a continuous, highly repetitive metal forming operation using a heavy industrial press machine. The primary process involves feeding flat, unshaped metal pieces into the machine, operating the press to stamp or bend the parts, and clearing the finished components from the die. Supporting processes include material handling (restocking raw parts from bulk containers and transporting finished parts to bulk collection carts), manual bin leveling, occasional die maintenance, and quality assurance consultations with adjacent coworkers.

Detailed Process Breakdown

**Workstation Setup and Tooling**
The worker operates a yellow and black industrial press. Raw, unformed metal pieces are typically staged on the left side of the workstation, either in a small blue plastic bin or in loose piles directly on the work table. The worker utilizes various specialized hand tools—including a small magnetic ring worn on the finger, a cylindrical object, and a rectangular pusher—to safely manipulate the metal parts without placing their hands directly under the press mechanism.

**The Pressing Cycle**
The primary operation follows a strict, rapid sequence:
1. **Loading:** The worker uses their left hand to retrieve an unformed metal part from the staging area. Occasionally, they hold a stack of parts in their left hand and feed them one by one. The part is placed onto the lower die of the machine.
2. **Aligning:** Using the specialized tool in their right hand, the worker makes micro-adjustments to push or align the part perfectly within the die.
3. **Actuation:** The machine is triggered to cycle. Depending on the specific setup, this is done either by simultaneously pressing two green safety buttons on a control panel or via a mechanism engaged by the right-hand tool. A yellow safety guard rapidly drops down, and the machine actuates, stamping or bending the metal piece. 
4. **Clearing:** Once the guard lifts, the finished part is removed. The worker either pulls it out by hand, sweeps it aside, or uses a magnetic tool to flick it into a local catch bin, tray, or growing pile on the immediate work surface.

**Material Handling and Transport**
* **Restocking Raw Materials:** As the local supply depletes, the worker pauses the machine to restock. They bend down to scoop handfuls of unformed parts from large cardboard boxes or plastic-lined bins on the floor, transferring them to the work table. Occasionally, they swap an empty blue bin for a full one retrieved from a nearby pallet. During restocking, the worker is sometimes seen vigorously sifting and mixing the unformed parts in the bulk bins.
* **Moving Finished Parts:** The immediate work surface has limited capacity for finished parts. Once the local tray, blue bin, or table pile is full, the worker halts the press. They physically carry the small bin, the tray, or literal handfuls of finished parts across the aisle and dump them into large, floor-level metal carts or plastic-lined collection bins.

**Bin Leveling**
A highly frequent sub-task is manual bin leveling. Because finished parts pile up in the center of the large collection carts, the worker regularly stops pressing, turns to the large carts, and uses their bare hands or tools to push, spread, and flatten the accumulated metal parts. This distributes the parts more evenly and makes room for incoming batches. The worker also frequently pushes and pulls the heavy metal carts to adjust their positioning on the floor.

**Quality Checks and Coordination**
The worker routinely inspects the stamped components. They frequently pause to examine individual pieces, sometimes comparing a newly stamped part directly against older parts in the collection bins. There is extensive coordination with nearby coworkers; the worker is repeatedly seen showing parts to others, demonstrating the use of their hand tools, gesturing toward part bins, and exchanging small clear plastic bags containing metal parts or paperwork. 

**Machine Maintenance**
Periodically, the worker performs minor housekeeping at the machine, such as using their fingers to pick small debris out of the die mechanism or wiping down the control panel near the actuation buttons.

Possible Process Optimizations

* **Point-of-Use Material Presentation:** The worker frequently halts production to bend over and scoop raw parts from floor-level boxes. Elevating these bulk supply bins using spring-loaded lift tables, tilted staging racks, or gravity-fed hoppers would eliminate ergonomic strain and drastically reduce the time spent manually transferring parts to the work surface.
* **Direct-Drop Finished Goods Routing:** The current process requires batching finished parts on the table and manually walking them to a bulk cart. Modifying the workstation layout to include a gravity chute or a small incline conveyor could route finished parts directly from the die area into the large collection carts, eliminating the transport step.
* **Automated Part Ejection (Inference):** The worker manually extracts or sweeps away every finished piece. It is inferred that installing an automated ejection system—such as pneumatic air blow-off nozzles synchronized with the press cycle or an angled die bed—could allow finished parts to fall automatically into a catch bin. This would remove the manual clearing step and shorten the overall cycle time.
* **Passive Bin Leveling Systems (Inference):** The worker loses significant production time manually spreading finished parts inside the bulk carts so they do not overflow in the center. It is inferred that utilizing wider, shallower collection bins, or installing angled deflector plates inside the carts, could force the falling parts to distribute evenly on their own.
* **Physical Part Feeding Guides (Inference):** The worker uses specialized hand tools on every cycle to micro-adjust and properly seat the raw part on the die. It is inferred that adding fixed physical guide rails, nesting pins, or a gravity-fed magazine track to the die could guarantee immediate, perfect alignment as soon as the part is dropped, removing the need for manual tool-assisted positioning.

Evidence Limits

* Camera angles and rapid movements sometimes obscure the exact mechanism of the press actuation, making it difficult to confirm if the right-hand tool mechanically triggers the press or if it is purely for alignment and safety.
* The lack of audio prevents confirmation of the exact nature of the quality discussions with coworkers, including whether parts are defective, if specifications are being changed, or if routine training is occurring.
* The contents of the small clear plastic bags exchanged with coworkers are not visible enough to identify whether they contain quality control samples, specialized components, or documentation.
* It is not visually evident why the worker must occasionally vigorously mix and sift the raw, unformed parts in the bulk bins before transferring them to the machine.
