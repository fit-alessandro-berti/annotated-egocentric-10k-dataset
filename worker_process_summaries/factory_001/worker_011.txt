Process Summary

The observed operation is a single-station metal stamping and bending process. The worker operates a metal pressing machine to continuously convert flat metal blanks into bent, finished pieces. The workflow is highly repetitive and involves a manual feed and extraction cycle, punctuated by brief maintenance, material replenishment, and scrap disposal tasks.

Detailed Process Breakdown

**Workstation Layout**
The workstation is centered around a metal pressing machine with a central die. Flat, raw metal blanks are stored in a bin situated low and to the worker's left. The left side of the machine bed serves as a staging area for stacks of these blanks. To the right of the machine is a plastic-lined bin for finished parts. Further to the right, on the floor, is a blue plastic crate used for scrap metal collection.

**Core Stamping Cycle**
The primary task follows a strict, repeating manual loop:
1. The worker uses their left hand to pick up a flat metal piece from the staged stack on the left side of the machine bed.
2. The flat piece is placed into the central die.
3. The worker uses both hands to simultaneously press two green buttons. This is a standard two-hand safety interlock that activates the press and prevents hands from being in the die area during operation.
4. After the machine cycles, the worker uses their right hand to remove the bent, finished piece.
5. The finished piece is placed into the plastic-lined bin on the right.

**Material Replenishment**
Because the machine processes parts rapidly, the staging area on the machine bed frequently depletes. To replenish it, the worker regularly reaches down into the lower bin on their left, picks up heavy stacks of flat metal blanks with both hands, lifts them, and places them onto the machine bed. Occasionally, the worker will slide these heavy stacks closer to the active central die area to maintain an ergonomic reaching distance. At times, the worker taps the stack of blanks in their hands to properly align them before feeding the machine.

**Scrap and Debris Management**
Scrap metal accumulates in a tray located behind the main die. Periodically, the worker halts the primary pressing cycle to pull this tray forward. They gather a handful of scrap manually, walk a few steps to the right, drop the scrap into the blue floor crate, and return to the machine. Additionally, the worker occasionally uses a hose-attached air gun to blow compressed air over the machine bed to clear away debris. 

**Machine Adjustments and Pauses**
The worker occasionally pauses to manually adjust metal blocks and die components in the central machine area, ensuring proper seating for the pressing cycle. There are also brief moments where the worker steps away from the machine and walks across the factory floor before returning, though the exact purpose of these trips (e.g., retrieving items off-camera, taking a brief physical break, or checking with personnel) is not visible.

Possible Process Optimizations

*   **Material Presentation (Lift Table/Cart):** The worker frequently bends down to lift heavy stacks of metal blanks from a low bin. Providing a spring-loaded pallet lift, an adjustable scissor lift table, or a raised staging cart for the raw materials would keep the blanks at waist or bed height. This would reduce physical strain and eliminate the time taken to bend and lift the materials.
*   **Scrap Chute and Bin Relocation:** Currently, the worker must stop production, pull a scrap tray, gather sharp scrap by hand, and walk to a disposal bin. This could be optimized by installing a tilted scrap chute behind the die that automatically funnels offcuts into a bin located directly beneath or beside the machine. At a minimum, moving the blue scrap crate immediately next to the operator would eliminate the walking steps required for disposal.
*   **Automated Part Ejection (Inference):** Based on the nature of the stamping press, it may be possible to implement a pneumatic auto-eject system. A timed burst of compressed air could blow the finished piece into the right-side bin the moment the die opens. This would eliminate the right-hand manual extraction step, allowing the worker to maintain a continuous left-hand loading rhythm and significantly increasing the cycle speed.
*   **Automated Debris Clearing:** Instead of the worker pausing to pick up a manual air gun, an automated air blow-off nozzle could be integrated into the machine's cycle. This would clear the die of debris automatically on every upstroke, reducing machine idle time and preventing potential part defects caused by seated debris.
