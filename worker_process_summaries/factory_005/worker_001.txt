Detailed Process Breakdown

Motor Assembly Finishing and Packaging
The worker is responsible for final touches on a batch of blue water pump motors and preparing their packaging. 
- Material Preparation: The worker navigates the warehouse to gather flat cardboard sheets, carrying them to an aisle of open boxes lined with pink material. They manually fold the cardboard along pre-scored lines to form inner box inserts and place them into the open boxes. They also fetch a pallet jack, transport blue plastic pallets to the staging area, and retrieve a large roll of plastic film/bags.
- Motor Labeling and Plating: At a workbench, the worker peels and applies adhesive labels to blue motors, rubbing them to ensure adhesion. They then process a batch of ten motors in a repetitive loop: positioning a metallic nameplate on the motor housing, inserting small pins through the plate, and using a hammer to tap the pins securely into place.
- Packaging Setup: After moving between areas, the worker takes completed blue boxes to a strapping machine. They lift a box onto the machine, manually feed a plastic strap around it, and trigger the machine to secure two tight plastic bands. The strapped box is then moved to a nearby blue plastic pallet.

Automated Stator Winding and Assembly
This multi-stage process involves preparing copper coils, winding them into stator cores using a machine, and performing final manual electrical connections and finishing.
- Coil Tying: At a workbench covered in loose copper coils and bare stator cores, the worker manually threads white string through individual coils, tying knots to secure their looped shape.
- Machine Winding: The worker transports a tied coil and a bare stator core to an automated winding machine. They mount the coil onto the lower tooling fingers and slide the stator core onto the upper central shaft. The machine engages, pulling the coil into the stator. The worker unloads the wound stator and returns it to the bench, repeating this cycle multiple times.
- Finishing Connections: A batch of wound stators is moved to a finishing station equipped with a rotating fixture. The worker manually routes the colored lead wires (red, blue, black) through the core. They trim the wire ends with scissors and use a lighter to burn off the protective enamel coating. Insulating sleeves are slid over the wires, the exposed copper ends are twisted together with pliers, and a soldering iron is used to seal the joints.
- Lacing and Wedging: The stator is moved to a stand where the worker tightly laces the copper end turns with string, tying them securely. Using a flathead tool, they forcefully push white insulating wedges down into the stator slots over the wires. Excess paper and string are trimmed with scissors.
- Testing: The worker connects the stator’s lead wires to alligator clips on a testing panel, verifies the connection via indicator lights, and disconnects it. Final manual pressing of the copper windings ensures tight packing.

Semi-Automatic Stator Wire Insertion
At a different machine, the worker performs a highly manual wire-packing task. They mount a stator onto the machine's central fixture, pull copper wire from a feed, and thread it into position. During the automatic winding phase, the worker keeps their hands closely engaged, guiding the wire. When the machine stops, the worker uses a small pointed metal tool to pull large loops of copper wire, twist them, and pack them tightly into the stator slots. They repeatedly rotate the stator by hand during this process. After cutting the wire with scissors, they press a green button to finish the cycle.

Lathe Machining
The worker operates a manual lathe to perform facing operations on orange, wheel-like metal parts.
- Loading: The worker mounts an unmachined part into the lathe chuck, aligns it by hand, and uses a spanner to tighten multiple bolts on the chuck to secure the part. They spin the chuck manually to verify alignment before making final tightening adjustments.
- Machining: The worker engages the machine rotation and uses the carriage and cross-slide handwheels to bring the cutting tool to the part, machining the flat face. They make continuous minor handwheel adjustments during the cut, generating long metal shavings.
- Deburring and Cleaning: While the part spins, the worker frequently holds a small scraper tool against the inner edge to deburr it. They routinely use a small brush to sweep metal shavings off the tool post and carriage.
- Unloading and Material Handling: The worker stops the machine, uses the spanner to loosen the chuck bolts, and removes the part. Unmachined parts are picked up from nearby piles or the floor, and finished parts are often tossed onto the floor or placed in a bin.

Possible Process Optimizations

- Workholding Automation (Inference): The most evident bottleneck in the lathe process is the manual tightening and loosening of multiple chuck bolts using a spanner for every individual part. Upgrading the lathe to a pneumatic chuck, hydraulic chuck, or a quick-release collet system would significantly reduce machine idle time and operator effort during the load/unload cycles.
- Material Staging and Presentation: During the lathe operations, the worker is observed picking up parts from the floor and throwing finished parts back onto the floor. Presenting unmachined parts at waist-height in gravity-fed bins and providing a dedicated, elevated catch-bin for finished parts would eliminate ergonomic strain, reduce handling time, and prevent potential damage to the machined surfaces.
- Tool Organization (5S): At the lathe, the worker frequently alternates between a spanner, an Allen key, a scraper, and a brush. Implementing a localized tool holder, shadow board, or retractable tethers directly on or adjacent to the lathe carriage would reduce tool searching and reaching time.
- Wire Stripping Method (Inference): Using a lighter flame to burn off the enamel coating on copper wires during stator finishing is a slow and potentially inconsistent process. Introducing a mechanical rotary wire stripper, a chemical stripping dip, or a thermal stripping pot would improve cycle time, ensure uniform stripping, and reduce safety hazards.
- Workstation Layout and Transport: The worker spends significant time walking across the facility to retrieve pallets, pallet jacks, cardboard, plastic film, and various product batches. Reorganizing the floor plan to position packaging materials closer to the final assembly area, or establishing a dedicated material handling role to supply workstations, would reduce non-value-added transport time.
- Batch Sizing and Flow (Inference): The stator winding process features heavily batched steps (e.g., tying all coils, then machining all coils). Balancing the manual coil tying and finishing steps with the automated winding machine's cycle time could allow for single-piece flow or smaller batch processing, reducing work-in-progress inventory staging on the workbenches.
