Process Summary

The worker primarily operates a large drill press to machine the center holes of flanged cast metal parts. This core responsibility is accompanied by manual part inspection using gauges, hole deburring, and extensive material handling using pallet jacks and manual carrying techniques. The worker operates in two distinct modes: an independent, high-volume drilling cycle, and a highly collaborative cycle involving a handoff with an adjacent grinding station. Secondary duties include operating a smaller stamping or tapping machine for rectangular components, retrieving processing fluids, and occasionally interacting with outdoor storage and transport equipment.

Detailed Process Breakdown

Core Drilling Cycle
The standard operational loop begins with the worker retrieving an unmachined cast metal part from a stacked supply or a nearby cart on their right. The worker places the part flat onto the drill press bed and aligns the center hole beneath the machine's spindle. Using their right hand, they pull down a side lever to lower the rotating cutting tool into the part while holding the part steady. Once the drilling is complete, the worker raises the lever, removes the part from the bed, and transitions to inspection or deburring before placing the finished part into a wire mesh bin, a large metal container, or onto a stacked pile on a metal table to their left.

Inspection and Deburring
Immediately following the drilling step, the worker applies a variety of finishing and quality-check steps before discarding the part:
*   **Gauging:** The worker frequently uses a cylindrical plug gauge to verify the internal diameter of the newly machined hole, or a flat rectangular block gauge to check the top surface around the hole. These gauges are manually wiped or pressed against the part.
*   **Deburring/Cleaning:** During certain production runs, the worker uses a small metal hand scraper or a piece of sandpaper to clean and deburr the inner edges of the newly machined hole. 
*   The application of these checks is intermittent; some parts receive plug gauge checks, some receive surface block checks, some are deburred, and some are simply drilled and binned with no immediate secondary action. 

Collaborative Grinding Loop
In a distinct variation of the workflow, the worker acts as both a material handler and downstream processor for a nearby coworker operating a grinder. 
*   **Material Supply:** The worker gathers raw parts by threading a long metal bar through the center holes of multiple castings, carrying the heavy stack by hand, and depositing them at the grinder’s station.
*   **Waiting and Cleaning:** The worker waits at the grinder station while the coworker grinds a part. During this idle time, the worker frequently uses a brush or a cloth to sweep metal shavings and dust off the coworker's table.
*   **Processing:** Once the coworker finishes grinding a part, the worker picks it up, carries it back to their own drill press, drills the center hole, deburs it with a hand tool, and places it into the wire bin. They then immediately walk back to the grinder station to repeat the wait-and-retrieve cycle.

Material Handling and Logistics
The worker independently manages their workstation's bulk material flow. They use a manual yellow pallet jack to lift and transport heavily loaded wire mesh bins across the factory floor, occasionally navigating narrow aisles. They also swap out full bins for empty ones. When loading parts into empty, deep wire mesh bins, the worker must occasionally lean over the edge to manually arrange the parts so they stack neatly at the bottom. 
Other sporadic logistical tasks include walking across the factory to dip a small metal cup into a large tank of dark liquid and carrying it back to the workbench, transporting a single drilled component to a large lathe/milling machine elsewhere in the facility, and briefly leaving the building to manipulate a chain hoist on a parked white truck and inspect empty wire mesh racks outdoors.

Secondary Machine Operation
Briefly, the worker operates a separate, smaller machine (likely a stamping or tapping press). They place a small, flat rectangular metal part onto the machine bed, pull a handle down to actuate the tool, lift the handle, and remove the part. 

Possible Process Optimizations

*   **Workstation Decoupling and Buffering:** The collaborative loop with the grinding station features high machine idle time for the drill press and high worker idle time while waiting for the grinder to finish. *Inference:* Establishing a shared buffer table or a gravity roller chute between the two stations would allow the grinder to build up a small Work-In-Progress (WIP) queue. This would decouple the tasks, eliminating the need for the drill press operator to wait and repeatedly walk back and forth for single parts.
*   **Material Transport Method:** Moving raw stock by threading a metal bar through the center holes and carrying stacks by hand is highly manual. *Inference:* Supplying raw castings on specialized wheeled carts with vertical pegs, or utilizing a localized roller conveyor, would reduce manual lifting and transit times.
*   **Point-of-Use Fluid Storage:** The worker leaves the station to fetch dark liquid from a distant bulk tank using a small cup. *Inference:* Providing a dedicated, moderately sized dispenser of this liquid directly at the workbench would eliminate the transit time and reduce the spill risk associated with carrying an open cup across the factory floor.
*   **Ergonomic Bin Loading:** The worker currently drops parts into deep wire mesh bins and must lean inside to arrange them. *Inference:* Implementing spring-loaded bottom bins (which lower as weight is added) or drop-side wire bins would prevent the worker from having to reach deeply into the container to organize parts.
*   **Standardized Inspection/Deburring Fixtures:** The manual swapping between plug gauges, flat blocks, hand scrapers, and sandpaper requires picking up and setting down multiple small tools. *Inference:* Consolidating the gauging into a fixed go/no-go stand, or integrating a deburring brush step directly into the drill press cycle (e.g., a secondary spindle or static brush fixture), could smooth out the repetitive handling.
