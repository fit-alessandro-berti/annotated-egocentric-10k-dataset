Process Summary
The worker performs a repetitive machining operation using a manual drill press to modify red, cast-metal parts. The core workflow consists of retrieving raw materials from a nearby basket, positioning a guide plate onto the part, manually drilling the part, and subsequently walking to transport the finished piece to a floor-level staging pile. The worker processes these parts sequentially in a continuous loop.

Detailed Process Breakdown
- Material Setup: The worker begins each cycle by retrieving a single unmachined red cast-metal part from a wire basket located to the left of the drill press. They place the part onto the machine's circular, slotted table. To align the tooling, they place a square silver guide plate on top of the red part and secure it using a silver alignment pin.
- Machining Operation: With the piece assembled, the worker holds the part steady with their left hand. Using their right hand, they repeatedly pull down a large side lever to lower the rotating spindle and drill into the part. During one cycle, the worker was observed briefly picking up a wrench to adjust a fitting on the setup before resuming the drilling action.
- Disassembly and Material Handling: Once drilling is complete, the worker disassembles the setup by removing the alignment pin and the silver guide plate. They pick up the finished part, walk away from the drill press, and place the part onto the floor next to a growing pile of identical finished parts. They then walk back to the machine to begin the next cycle.
- Interruptions and Interactions: The primary workflow is highly repetitive but features occasional minor interruptions. In one instance, a dropped alignment pin forces the worker to stop, step back, and bend down to retrieve it from the floor before continuing. In another instance, the worker temporarily suspends operations to walk over and observe a coworker at a nearby metal table handling a large, rusty metal tray, before returning to the drill press station.

Possible Process Optimizations
- Workstation Layout and Material Staging: The most significant visible inefficiency is the transportation of finished parts. The worker walks away from the machine and bends down to place a single finished part on the floor during every cycle. Placing an elevated receiving bin, cart, or staging table immediately adjacent to the right side of the drill press would eliminate this repetitive walking and bending, vastly reducing cycle time and unnecessary motion. 
- Fixture/Jig Design: The worker manually holds the part steady with their left hand while operating the drill press with their right, utilizing a loose guide plate and alignment pin. Inference: Installing a fixed toggle clamp, vice, or a permanent jig bolted directly to the slotted table would eliminate the need to manually grip the part during machining. Furthermore, a hinged or fixed drill bushing setup could eliminate the constant manual assembly and disassembly of the loose guide plate and alignment pin.
- Batching Strategy: Instead of transporting parts one-by-one to a floor pile, implementing a local catch-bin at the workstation would allow for batch transport. Parts could be moved by the bin-load, significantly improving machine utilization time.
