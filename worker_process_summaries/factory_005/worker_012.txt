Process Summary
The worker is primarily engaged in a continuous machining operation using a metal lathe to cut the faces and inside edges of small metal parts. A secondary, intermittent process involves the worker leaving their primary workstation to assist a neighboring operator by holding a tool against a spinning workpiece on a larger lathe. 

Detailed Process Breakdown
The worker’s operations are split between their primary machining tasks and occasional assistance at a secondary station.

Primary Machining Cycle:
- Material Retrieval: The worker reaches down to a metal bin located on the floor to pick up an unmachined metal part.
- Loading: The worker presses the unmachined part directly onto the lathe's fixture/chuck while it is actively spinning.
- Machining: The worker operates the carriage and cross-slide handwheels to guide the cutting tool into the metal, machining the face and inside edge. 
- Tool Adjustment: Frequently, the worker pauses mid-cut to adjust the machine's toolpost using a hand wrench before resuming the cut with the handwheels.
- Unloading: Once the cut is complete, the tool is retracted, and the worker pulls the finished part off the continuously spinning fixture.
- Staging: The worker walks over to place the finished part onto a stack located on the shop floor.
- Quality Check: On one occasion, the worker uses manual calipers to measure the dimensions of a completed part.

Secondary Assisting Process:
- Several times during the observed period, the worker halts their primary machining cycle and steps over to a nearby large lathe operated by a second worker.
- The worker reaches into the secondary machine's workspace to manually hold a tool or object against a heavy, spinning metal part to clean or finish it.
- After completing this assist, the worker returns to their own lathe to resume their primary cycle.

Possible Process Optimizations
- Material Staging and Layout: Raw materials are stored in a bin on the floor, and finished parts are stacked on the floor. Moving both incoming and outgoing parts to waist-height staging tables or carts situated immediately next to the lathe would reduce walking, bending, and the overall time required for loading and unloading.
- Tooling Setup: The worker repeatedly stops mid-cycle to adjust the toolpost with a wrench. Upgrading to a more rigid tooling setup, utilizing a quick-change toolpost, or standardizing the tool position during the initial setup phase could eliminate these in-cycle interruptions and create a smoother, continuous cut.
- Decoupling Operations (Inference): The periodic requirement for the worker to assist a neighboring machine operator creates cross-station dependency and forces idle time at the primary lathe. It is inferred that designing a fixed tool holder or jig for the secondary machine would allow the second operator to independently finish their parts, thus decoupling the two workstations and improving throughput for both.
- Inspection Station Layout (Inference): Because the caliper check is performed while simply holding the part in hand, creating a dedicated, small inspection shelf with a designated space for the calipers could standardize the inspection process and prevent tools from being misplaced among the metal chips.
